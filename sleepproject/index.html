HOW TO SLEEP
BETTER AT NIGHT

Introduction

Today's world is vast with opportunities. We have schools to attend, jobs to maintain, hobbies to indulge and dreams to achieve. While all of these things are being juggled everyday, it is easy to overlook your own well-being and general health.
People associate good health with regular workout routines and your diet measured down to the milligram, with the perfect balance between fat, protein and carbs. And while this is definitely true, people often forget the last important part of the healthy trio: Sleep.
Something as simple as catching some good ZZZ’s will slowly bring positive change, not only within your body physically, but to your mental well-being, and here are 5 pieces of advice to help optimize your sleep.

Our Tips

  • 1. Consider your alcohol consumption

    More than a couple of drinks at night decreases your overall sleep quality. Besides the fact more bathroom visits are inevitable, it slows some of the brain's functions to produce hormones and messes with your inner alarm clock. Consuming a heavy meal before bed also isn't advised. Having all that food for the body to focus on digesting instead of using that energy to recharge your batteries and replenish the body's functions. Once in a while won't tip the scales, but extended periods of over consumption will also result in an aggravation of your body's nightly rhythm.

  • 2. Optimize your bedroom

    Your bedroom is vital for optimization of a good night’s sleep. This includes everything from external light sources and the temperature, even the arrangement of the furniture in your rooms has proven to impact your sleep schedule. Also, daily ventilation of the bedroom (if possible) and regularly switching the bedsheets improves the overall quality of the sleep. For most people, the optimal temperature when sleeping lies around 20*C.

  • 3. Relax in the evening before bedtime

    An hour leading up to your bedtime, spend the time however way makes you relax. Listen to music, read a book, draw, knit, meditate. Getting a routine up and going will allow your body to recognize bedtime easier, boosting the effects. Avoid looking too much into the generic lights from screens, as it disrupts the circadian rhythm you are creating for yourself. A small workout or walk are also viable options, as long as you are getting to a relaxed state of mind.

  • 4. Exercise regularly - But not right before bed

    Even though a small workout session or walk benefits sleep, building up too much of a sweat can have the opposite effect on your body. There is no doubt, exercising regularly improves your health and benefits your sleeping schedule. Many of the consequences following sleep deprivation, such as sleep apnea and insomnia, have reduced impact on people with a workout routine. Especially outdoor exercising in the sun is a recipe for success, and even if workout before bedtime is not recommended, getting a workout is still beneficial.

  • 5. Get a rhythm for yourself, your brain will appreciate it!

    Your body has a built-in clock that tries to align itself with the sunrise and sunset. This is called a circadian rhythm, and having a stable one can aid long term sleep quality. The more consistently you go to bed and wake up at the same time, pattern forms inside your brain, helping recognize sleep is near and setting the internal alarm for the next day. Studies also highlight an irregular sleep pattern and going late to bed alters the body levels of melatonin, a hormone created in your brain responsible for your circadian rhythm. After several weeks of repetition, you might not even need the use of an alarm clock!
